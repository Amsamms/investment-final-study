1 · What is a bond? (assume you know nothing)

Forget every finance word for a minute. Picture this:

Your neighbor needs $1,000. He says: "Lend me the money. Every year for 10 years I'll pay you $100 in interest. At the end of those 10 years I'll give your $1,000 back."

You agree. He writes the deal on a piece of paper and signs it. That piece of paper is a bond.

That's the whole concept. A bond is just a written IOU. (IOU is informal English shorthand for "I Owe You" — a signed piece of paper acknowledging that one person borrowed money from another and promises to pay it back. Pronounced as three letters: "I-O-U.") The IOU says "I borrowed money from you, here's what I promise to pay back, and here's when."

The only difference between a neighbor's IOU and a real-world bond is that real bonds can be sold — if next year you want your money back early, you can sell the IOU to someone else, and the company will then pay them the future $100 coupons and the final $1,000 par value.

Companies and governments issue bonds when they need to borrow large amounts of money. Investors buy bonds because they want a relatively safe, predictable income stream.

Now let's name every piece of that IOU, one by one

Each term below is a piece of the same neighbor story. Read them in order — each one builds on the previous.

a · Par value (also called Face value, or "M")

Definition: the original loan amount — the dollar amount printed on the front of the bond. This is what gets paid back at the very end.

In our story: $1,000. That's the size of the loan, and that's what your neighbor will hand back at the end of year 10.

Convention for this course: unless a problem tells you otherwise, assume par value = $1,000. Almost every textbook problem uses this number. Symbol: M.

Important: par value is NOT the same as the bond's price. The price changes every day in the market; the par value is fixed forever (it's printed on the bond).

b · Coupon interest rate & the dollar coupon (INT)

Definition: a fixed percentage written on the bond that tells you how much interest gets paid each year. Multiply this percentage by par value to get the actual dollar amount you receive — that dollar amount is called the dollar coupon or just "INT".

In our story: coupon rate = 10% (because $100/year on a $1,000 loan = 10%). Dollar coupon = $100/year.

Why it's called a "coupon": historically, bonds came as physical paper certificates with little tear-off slips ("coupons") around the edges. Every year you'd cut off a coupon and mail it to the company to claim your interest. The name stuck even though the paper coupons are long gone.

Critical point: the coupon rate is fixed for life. Once a bond is issued at "10% coupon," that 10% never changes — even if interest rates in the market rise to 15% or fall to 3%. This rigidity is the source of all the price-vs-yield drama you'll see later in the chapter.

c · Maturity (and the maturity date)

Definition: maturity is the day the bond's life ends — when the borrower must repay the par value and the bond ceases to exist. "Maturity" is also used loosely as a synonym for "time remaining until that day" (e.g., "this bond has 10 years to maturity").

In our story: maturity is at the end of year 10. On that day your neighbor hands you the $1,000 and the deal is done.

It shrinks every year. A bond issued in 2025 with 10 years to maturity will, in 2026, only have 9 years to maturity. By 2035 it has 0 years to maturity — that's the maturity date itself.

"At maturity" = "on the maturity date" = "at the end of year N". Every time you see one of those phrases in this chapter, picture: the bond's life is over, the issuer pays $1,000 (par value), and you walk away.

d · Issue date

Definition: the day the bond was first sold by the issuer (the borrower). Maturity = issue date + bond's stated life. Mostly just trivia — appears occasionally on multiple-choice questions to confuse you with the maturity date.

e · Issuer vs Bondholder

Two roles in every bond:

  • Issuer = the borrower (the company or government that needs the money and wrote the IOU). Pays the coupons and the par value. In our story: your neighbor.
  • Bondholder / investor = the lender (whoever currently owns the IOU). Receives the coupons and par value. In our story: you.

If you sell the bond to someone else mid-life, they become the bondholder and start receiving the future cash flows.

f · Required rate of return (kd) — the market's mood

Definition: the yield that today's investors demand to be willing to lend money to a borrower of this risk level. It's the market's current mood, not a property of the bond itself.

Think of it as: "what return would I need to be tempted to put my money into a bond like this today?" If similar bonds are paying 7% in the market right now, then kd = 7%. If next month the economy heats up and similar bonds are paying 9%, then kd = 9%.

Critical contrast: the coupon rate is fixed forever (printed on the bond). The required rate (kd) changes every day with the market. The mismatch between these two numbers is what causes a bond's price to move up and down. Symbol: k_d (the "d" stands for "debt").

g · Three quick price labels — par, premium, discount

You'll see these three words constantly throughout the chapter. They describe the bond's price relative to its $1,000 par value:

  • Par bond — price = $1,000 (price equals par value).
  • Premium bond — price > $1,000 (price is above par; the buyer pays a "premium" because the coupon is generous).
  • Discount bond — price < $1,000 (price is below par; the buyer demands a "discount" because the coupon is stingy compared to current market rates).

The reason any bond ends up in one of these three buckets is explained in §2 — but for now, just know that par / premium / discount are simply labels for "is the price equal to / above / below $1,000."

h · Yield to Maturity (YTM)

Definition: the actual return you'll earn if you buy the bond at today's price and hold it all the way to maturity (no early sale). It's a single percentage that summarizes "your real return on the deal."

How it differs from kd: kd is what the market demands; YTM is what you'll actually earn. In a fairly priced market, they're equal. (We'll treat them as the same number in this course.)

Also called "promised yield" because it's the return the issuer is promising you, assuming they don't default.

i · Call provision (a clause some bonds have)

Definition: a clause in the bond contract that gives the issuer (the borrower) the right to buy back the bond early — at a stated "call price" — before maturity. The bondholder has no say.

Why borrowers want it: if interest rates drop after they issued the bond at, say, 10%, they can call back the expensive 10% bond and re-borrow at the new lower rate (e.g., 6%). Saves them money. Hurts you.

Consequence: because callable bonds are riskier for the lender, callable bonds offer higher coupon rates than non-callable bonds — investors won't buy them otherwise.

Most call clauses include a "deferred call" (e.g., the bond can't be called for the first 5 years) and a "declining call premium" (the call price starts above par and shrinks toward par as time passes — small consolation prize for the bondholder).

Classic exam trap: "A call provision gives bondholders the right to demand repayment." → FALSE. The call provision belongs to the issuer, not the bondholder. (When the bondholder has the right to force early repayment, that's a separate clause called a put provision — much rarer.)
j · Sinking fund (another optional clause)

Definition: a clause requiring the issuer to retire portions of the bond gradually instead of waiting to repay everything at maturity. Like an ordinary loan amortization, where you pay down a chunk of principal every year.

Effect on the bondholder: reduces risk (you get principal back along the way, not just at the end) and shortens the bond's average life. But: if rates have fallen by then, you're forced to reinvest those early-returned dollars at the new lower rate, which is bad for you.

How the issuer satisfies a sinking fund — two methods:

  • Call x% at par — pick some bonds at random and pay $1,000 each. Used when kd < coupon (bond is at a premium → calling at $1,000 is a bargain for the issuer because the market price is higher).
  • Buy on the open market — go to the market and purchase bonds at their current discount price. Used when kd > coupon (bond is at a discount → buying it cheap on the market beats paying $1,000).

The issuer always picks whichever path costs less.

k · Three more terms you'll see in the formula (preview)
  • Cash flow = any payment you receive. For a bond, the cash flows are the yearly coupons + the final par-value lump sum.
  • Annuity = a series of equal payments at regular intervals (every year, every month, etc.). The bond's coupons form an annuity: $100 every year for 10 years.
  • Lump sum = a single one-time payment. The par value paid at maturity is a lump sum: $1,000 once, at the end.
  • Present value (PV) = how much a future amount of money is worth today, after adjusting for the fact that money in the future is worth less than money now (because you could have invested today's money to grow it). PV is the engine of the bond formula in §2.

2 · How much is a bond worth? (the master formula)

2a · The "time value of money" — the one idea behind everything

Before any formula, here's the single concept that makes bond valuation work:

$1 today is worth more than $1 a year from now. Why? Because if you had the $1 today, you could put it in a bank earning, say, 10% — and a year later it would be $1.10. So $1 today = $1.10 a year from now. Equivalently: $1.10 a year from now is worth only $1 today.

This is called the time value of money. The "shrinking" of a future amount back to today's equivalent value is called discounting. The interest rate used to do that shrinking is called the discount rate.

The formula: if a future amount of $X arrives t years from now, and the discount rate is r, then today's value (the present value, PV) is:

$$ \text{PV} \;=\; \frac{X}{(1+r)^{\,t}} $$
XThe future amount (in dollars) rThe discount rate per period (e.g., 0.10 for 10%) tHow many periods in the future X arrives
Sanity check the PV formula

If your bank pays 10% per year, what's $1.10 (received in 1 year) worth today?

PV = 1.10 / (1 + 0.10)^1 = 1.10 / 1.10 = $1.00

✅ Matches our intuition. What about $100 received in 10 years at 10%?

PV = 100 / (1 + 0.10)^10 = 100 / 2.5937 = $38.55

So a promise of $100 a decade from now is worth only $38.55 to you today, given a 10% discount rate. Money far in the future is heavily discounted — this is why the very last coupon a 10-year bond pays is "worth almost nothing" in today's dollars.

2b · A bond is just a series of future payments — discount each one

Now apply that to a bond. The bondholder receives:

  1. An annuity of coupons (a series of equal payments) — INT every year for N years.
  2. A lump sum (one-time payment) of par value M at year N.

The fair price of the bond is the sum of the present values of all those payments. Discount each one back to today, then add them up.

2c · Reading the Σ symbol (sigma)

Math books write "add these things up" using the Greek capital sigma Σ. It looks intimidating but it's just shorthand. Σ from t=1 to N of (something with t) means: "plug in t=1, then t=2, then t=3, …, all the way to t=N, and add the results." That's it. Don't let the symbol scare you — it's just a "+" repeated.

2d · The master formula

Put it all together: bond price = PV of every coupon + PV of par.

$$ V_B \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_{t=1}^{N} \frac{\text{INT}}{(1+k_d)^{\,t}}}_{\text{PV of all coupons (annuity)}} \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{M}{(1+k_d)^{\,N}}}_{\text{PV of par (lump sum at the end)}} $$
V_BBond's fair price today (the "value of the bond") INTDollar coupon per period (= coupon rate × par value) MPar value (usually $1,000) k_dRequired rate of return per period — the market's current demanded yield (and our discount rate) NTotal number of periods until maturity tIndex that runs through each period, 1, 2, 3, …, N

Read it out loud: "The bond is worth the present value of every coupon I'll receive, plus the present value of the par value I'll get at the end." That's all there is.

2e · The annuity shortcut (so you don't sum 10 fractions by hand)

Adding up INT/(1+kd)¹ + INT/(1+kd)² + … + INT/(1+kd)N by hand for a 30-year bond would take forever. Luckily there's a closed-form shortcut for the sum of an equal-payment annuity:

$$ \text{PV of annuity} \;=\; \text{INT} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+k_d)^{-N}}{k_d} $$

This is the annuity factor. You don't need to memorize the derivation — just know it gives you "the PV of getting INT every period for N periods."

So the practical version of the master formula becomes:

$$ V_B \;=\; \text{INT} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+k_d)^{-N}}{k_d} \;+\; \frac{M}{(1+k_d)^{\,N}} $$

This is the form you'll actually plug numbers into. The three worked examples below do exactly that.

Example 1 · Bond at par

10-year, 10% coupon, $1,000 par, kd = 10%. Find VB.

INT = 0.10 × 1000 = $100/year N = 10, k_d = 0.10, M = $1,000 V_B = 100·[ (1 − (1+0.10)^−10) / 0.10 ] + 1000 / (1+0.10)^10 = 100 · 6.1446 + 1000 · 0.3855 = $614.46 + $385.54 = $1,000.00

Coupon rate = kd, so price = par. Notice it splits exactly into the slide's $614.46 annuity + $385.54 lump sum.

Example 2 · Inflation rises → discount

Same bond, but kd jumps to 13%.

V_B = 100·[ (1 − 1.13^−10) / 0.13 ] + 1000 / 1.13^10 = 100 · 5.4262 + 1000 · 0.2946 = $542.62 + $294.59 = $837.21

Price < par. When the market demands more (13%) than the bond pays (10%), buyers will only take it at a discount.

Example 3 · Inflation falls → premium

Same bond, but kd drops to 7%.

V_B = 100·[ (1 − 1.07^−10) / 0.07 ] + 1000 / 1.07^10 = 100 · 7.0236 + 1000 · 0.5083 = $702.36 + $508.35 = $1,210.71

Price > par. The 10% coupon is "too generous" vs. the 7% market rate, so investors bid the price up.

3 · "Pull to par" — what happens to the price as time passes

So far we've computed prices at a single moment (today, with 10 years left). But what does the price do over time? If kd stays constant, the price slowly drifts toward par ($1,000) as the maturity date approaches. This drift is called "pull to par."

Why is it called that? Imagine the par value M = $1,000 is a magnet at the maturity date. No matter where the bond's price starts, the magnet pulls it toward $1,000 as the date gets closer — because on the day of maturity the price must equal par: that's literally the cash you receive.

Three flavors of drift

  • kd = 7% (premium bond) — starts at $1,372 when 30 years remain. Drifts down to $1,000 over time.
  • kd = 10% (par bond) — sits at $1,000 the whole time (no drift; coupon and discount rate match exactly).
  • kd = 13% (discount bond) — starts at $775 when 30 years remain. Climbs up to $1,000 over time.
Bond value over time, assuming kd stays constant. All three curves converge to $1,000 at maturity. The x-axis is "years remaining to maturity" — read it left-to-right (30 years left → 0 years left).
Why does this happen mathematically? Look at the master formula: as N shrinks (fewer years left), two things change:
  • The PV of the par lump sum grows toward $1,000 (because we're dividing by a smaller power of (1+kd) — less discounting).
  • The PV of the remaining coupons shrinks (fewer coupons left to discount).
These two effects cancel out perfectly only when coupon = kd. When they're not equal, the lump-sum effect wins and the price slides toward $1,000.

Important: "pull to par" assumes kd stays the same over the bond's life. In reality kd moves around, and that movement causes the bigger price swings (covered next, in §5).

4 · Yield to Maturity (YTM) — what return are you actually earning?

So far we've used the master formula in one direction: given a discount rate (kd), compute the price. But in real life you usually face the opposite question: "the bond is selling for $887 right now — if I buy it, what return will I earn?"

That return is called Yield to Maturity (YTM). It's the same formula, run backwards: instead of plugging in a rate to get the price, you plug in the price and solve for the rate.

4a · IRR — the math name for "yield to maturity"

If you've heard the term Internal Rate of Return (IRR), that's exactly what YTM is. IRR is a general finance concept: the discount rate that makes the present value of an investment's cash flows equal to its cost. For a bond, IRR = YTM. For a project, IRR = "the project's return." Same idea, different label.

4b · The equation we're solving

$$ \text{Price} \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{N} \frac{\text{INT}}{(1+\text{YTM})^{\,t}} \;+\; \frac{M}{(1+\text{YTM})^{\,N}} $$

All the inputs except YTM are known: the price you'd pay, the coupon, the par value, the years to maturity. YTM is the only unknown. We solve for it.

Bad news: there's no algebraic shortcut to isolate YTM. You can't "do it on paper." It has to be solved by trial-and-error or by a computer.

Good news: every financial calculator and every spreadsheet has a built-in solver for it.

4c · How to solve it on a financial calculator

A "financial calculator" (HP 10bII, TI BA II Plus, etc.) has 5 dedicated keys for time-value-of-money problems:

KeyWhat it stands forFor our bond problem
NNumber of periodsYears to maturity (or 2× years if semi-annual)

PVPresent valueThe price you'd pay entered as a negative (cash going OUT)

Sign convention (the #1 source of mistakes): money you pay out is negative; money you receive is positive. The price you pay for the bond (PV) is negative; the coupons you receive (PMT) and the par value at maturity (FV) are positive.

4d · How to solve it in Excel / Google Sheets

The function is =RATE(N, PMT, PV, FV) — same five inputs, same sign convention.

Worked example · YTM of a discount bond

A 10-year, 9% annual coupon, $1,000 par bond is selling for $887. Find YTM.

Inputs: N = 10 PMT = 0.09 × 1000 = $90 (annual coupon — positive, money coming in) PV = −887 (price you pay — negative, money going out) FV = +1000 (par value at maturity — positive) Excel: =RATE(10, 90, −887, 1000) → 0.1091 → YTM = 10.91% Calculator: enter N=10, PMT=90, PV=−887, FV=1000, press CPT I/YR → 10.91

Sanity check: price ($887) is below par ($1,000), so this is a discount bond → coupon (9%) must be less than YTM. We got YTM = 10.91% > 9%. ✅ The numbers are consistent.

Same bond, but at a premium

Now the same 9% coupon, 10-year, $1,000 par bond sells for $1,134.20.

=RATE(10, 90, −1134.20, 1000) → 0.0708 → YTM = 7.08%

Sanity check: price > par → premium bond → coupon must be > YTM. We got YTM = 7.08% < 9%. ✅ Consistent.

Quick rule (always do this check):
  • Discount bond (price < par) → YTM > coupon rate
  • Par bond (price = par) → YTM = coupon rate
  • Premium bond (price > par) → YTM < coupon rate
If your computed YTM violates this, you punched in a sign wrong. Re-check.

4e · Decomposing YTM into two pieces

Where does your YTM actually come from? It's the sum of two effects you experience as a bondholder:

  • Current yield — the cash income (coupons) you get per year, expressed as a % of the price you paid. Like a stock's dividend yield.
  • Capital gains yield — the % change in the bond's price over the year. (Δ means "change in.") If the price climbs from $887 to $900, that's a positive capital gains yield; if it drifts down, it's negative.
$$ \text{Current Yield} \;=\; \frac{\text{Annual coupon}}{\text{Current price}} \qquad \text{Capital Gains Yield} \;=\; \frac{\Delta \text{Price}}{\text{Beginning price}} $$ $$ \boxed{\;\text{YTM} \;=\; \text{Current Yield} \;+\; \text{Capital Gains Yield}\;} $$

In words: your total annual return = the income you collect + the price appreciation. This identity is exact only over short windows in textbook problems, but the conceptual split is what gets tested.

Worked example · Split the 9% bond at $887

We just computed YTM = 10.91% above. Now decompose it:

Current Yield = $90 / $887 = 0.1015 = 10.15% Capital gains yld = YTM − Current Yield = 10.91% − 10.15% = 0.76%

What does the 0.76% mean? The bond was bought at $887 and will be redeemed at $1,000 at maturity — that's a $113 price gain spread over the bond's life. The 0.76%/year capital gain is exactly the "pull to par" effect you saw in §3 expressed as a yearly yield.

Sign of the cap-gains yield depends on premium vs. discount:
  • Discount (price < par) → price climbs to par → cap-gains positive → YTM > current yield.
  • Par (price = par) → price stays flat → cap-gains = 0 → YTM = current yield = coupon.
  • Premium (price > par) → price drifts down to par → cap-gains negative → YTM < current yield.
Sign rule for cap-gains yield:
  • Discount bond (price < par) → price climbs to par → cap-gains positive → YTM > current yield.
  • Premium bond (price > par) → price drifts down to par → cap-gains negative → YTM < current yield.
  • Par bond → cap-gains = 0 → YTM = current yield = coupon rate.

5 · Two flavors of bond risk (and the trade-off between them)

Bonds are often called "safe," but they carry two specific risks that show up on every exam. The trick: the two risks oppose each other — protecting yourself from one always exposes you to the other.

5a · Interest-rate risk (also called "price risk")

Definition: the risk that the bond's price will fall because the market's required rate (kd) rises after you buy it.

You already saw the mechanism in §2: if kd goes up, the bond's PV goes down (you're dividing by a bigger discount factor). If you have to sell the bond before maturity at that lower price, you take a loss.

Key fact: long-term bonds suffer much more from this risk than short-term bonds. Why? The far-away cash flows are exponentially more sensitive to the discount rate (you're dividing by (1+kd)large N, which changes a lot when kd moves).

Numerical demonstration — same 10% coupon, $1,000 par bond, but compare 1-year vs. 10-year maturity at three different kd levels:

kd1-year, 10% bond% change10-year, 10% bond% change
5% (rates fall)$1,047.62+4.8%$1,386.09+38.6%

Same coupon, same yield change — but the 10-year's price swings ~6× more in percentage terms. Long maturity is dangerous when rates move.

How the % changes are computed (so you can verify): Δ% = (new price − $1,000) ÷ $1,000. For instance, the 10-year at 5% gives ($1,386.09 − $1,000) / $1,000 = +38.6%.

Price vs. kd. The 10-year line is much steeper than the 1-year line — its slope is the bond's sensitivity to interest rates. Steeper line = more interest-rate risk.

5b · Reinvestment-rate risk

Definition: the risk that future cash you receive (coupons, or par at maturity) will have to be re-invested at lower rates than you'd hoped.

"Reinvest" just means: when a coupon arrives in your account, you'd typically not spend it — you'd put it back into another investment. The return on that next investment depends on whatever rates are available at the time. If rates have fallen, your future income falls.

Key fact: short-term bonds suffer more from this risk than long-term bonds. Why? With a short-term bond, you get all your money back quickly and have to redeploy it. With a long-term bond, your coupon rate is locked in for many more years before you face the reinvestment decision.

Concrete example: You have $500,000 in 1-year bonds yielding 10% → $50,000 of income this year. The bonds mature; you get your $500,000 back. But interest rates have collapsed to 3% in the meantime. Now your $500,000 only earns $15,000/year going forward. Income dropped 70%.

If instead you had locked into a 30-year, 10% bond, your $50,000/year would have been guaranteed for the full 30 years — even though the rate elsewhere in the market is now 3%.

5c · The trade-off — pick your poison

Here's the central insight that gets tested constantly: you cannot avoid both risks at once. Whatever you do to reduce one increases the other.

Bond typeInterest-rate (price) riskReinvestment-rate risk
Long-term bondsHighLow
Short-term bondsLowHigh
High-coupon bondsLower price riskHigher reinvestment risk
Low-coupon (or zero-coupon) bondsHigher price riskLower reinvestment risk

Why does coupon size flip the trade-off the same way maturity does? A high-coupon bond returns more cash earlier (big payments now); a low-coupon bond delays the cash. So in terms of when the cash arrives, a high-coupon bond behaves like a "shorter" bond, and a low-coupon bond behaves like a "longer" one. The extreme case is a zero-coupon bond (no coupons at all, just one big lump sum at maturity) — it has the maximum possible price risk and zero reinvestment risk on coupons.

Nothing is riskless. Eliminate price risk → you've increased reinvestment risk. Eliminate reinvestment risk → you've increased price risk. The exam loves to test this with a "True or False" claim that some bond is "completely safe."

6 · Two real-world wrinkles — semi-annual coupons & Yield to Call

Up to here we've assumed bonds pay coupons once a year. Most real bonds don't — they pay every 6 months. We also assumed the bond runs all the way to maturity. Many bonds get called early. This section deals with both of those wrinkles.

6a · What does "semi-annual" mean?

Semi-annual simply means "twice a year." A bond with a 10% semi-annual coupon and $1,000 par doesn't pay $100 once a year — it pays $50 every 6 months (half the annual coupon, twice as often). Total dollars per year are still $100, but the timing is different.

Because the cash flows are now spaced 6 months apart instead of 12, the master formula's "period" must be 6 months, not 1 year. That means we need to adjust three of the inputs.

6b · The semi-annual conversion (3 swaps)

Take any annual quantities given in a problem, and convert them to a 6-month basis:

Annual quantity→ Semi-annual equivalentWhy
Years to maturity (n)Number of periods N = 2nTwice as many payment periods in the same calendar time
Annual kdPer-period rate = kd / 2Each 6-month period earns half the annual rate (this is called the "nominal annual rate divided by m" convention — see 6e)
Annual coupon (INT)Per-period coupon = INT / 2Each payment is half the annual amount
Par value (M)UnchangedPar is paid once at maturity regardless of compounding frequency

That's it. Plug the new N, new periodic rate, and new periodic coupon into the same master formula. The math is identical; only the numbers got smaller-and-more-frequent.

Worked example · 10-yr, 10% semi-annual coupon, kd = 13%

Semi-annual conversions:

N = 2 × 10 = 20 periods (each one is 6 months) periodic k_d = 13% / 2 = 6.5% per period PMT = $100 / 2 = $50 per period M = $1,000 (unchanged) Plug into master formula: V_B = 50·[ (1 − 1.065^−20) / 0.065 ] + 1000 / 1.065^20 = 50 · 11.0185 + 1000 · 0.2838 = $550.93 + $283.80 = $834.72

Compare to the annual version at 13%: that gave $837.21. The semi-annual price is slightly lower (~$2.50 less) because compounding twice a year is fractionally more aggressive than once a year — see 6e for the EFF% explanation.

6c · What is "Yield to Call" (YTC)?

Recall (§1i): a callable bond gives the issuer the right to repurchase the bond early at a stated call price. The issuer will exercise that right when interest rates have fallen — they can refinance more cheaply.

So if you buy a callable bond when it's trading at a premium (which means kd < coupon rate), there's a strong chance you won't hold it to maturity. The issuer will call it on the first allowed call date. Your real return depends on the cash flows up to that call date, not the maturity date.

Yield to Call (YTC) is the YTM equivalent for that scenario: it's the IRR you earn assuming the bond gets called on its earliest call date and you receive the call price at that point.

$$ \text{Price} \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{N_c} \frac{\text{INT}}{(1+\text{YTC})^{\,t}} \;+\; \frac{\text{Call price}}{(1+\text{YTC})^{\,N_c}} $$
N_cNumber of periods until first call date (NOT maturity) Call priceThe stated price at which the issuer will buy the bond back. Often equal to par + a small "call premium" (e.g., $1,050 instead of $1,000) INTSame dollar coupon per period as before — coupons keep being paid right up until the call

Three differences from YTM:

  • Replace N (years to maturity) with Nc (years to first call).
  • Replace M (par value at maturity) with the call price (the cash you'd actually receive).
  • The dollar coupon and price stay the same; you just shorten the horizon and swap the final lump sum.
Worked example · YTC for a callable premium semi-annual bond

A 10-year, 10% semi-annual coupon, $1,000 par bond is selling for $1,135.90. It can be called in 4 years at $1,050. Find YTC.

Semi-annual setup: N_c = 2 × 4 = 8 periods PMT = $100 / 2 = $50 PV = −$1,135.90 FV = +$1,050 (call price, NOT par value!) =RATE(8, 50, −1135.90, 1050) → 0.03568 → 3.568% per 6-month period To express as a "nominal annual" rate (the convention quoted by brokers and used in this course): Nominal annual YTC = 3.568% × 2 = 7.137%

So YTC = 7.137% nominal. If you want the true annualized return that accounts for the half-year compounding, see 6e.

6d · When to compute YTC vs YTM

Rule of thumb for what you'll actually earn:
  • Premium bond + callable → call is likely (issuer wants to refinance) → expect YTC.
  • Par or discount bond → call is unlikely (issuer wouldn't voluntarily refinance into a more expensive coupon) → expect YTM.

6e · Nominal annual rate vs Effective annual rate (EFF%) cancelled — not on the exam

One last subtlety. When a problem says "kd = 13% semi-annual," it usually means the nominal annual rate — a quoted yearly figure that gets divided by the number of periods per year. So you use 13%/2 = 6.5% per 6-month period.

But "earning 6.5% twice a year" is not exactly the same as "earning 13% once a year." Because the first 6.5% gets reinvested and earns its own 6.5% in the second half of the year, you actually end up with slightly more than 13%. The true annualized return is called the effective annual rate (EFF%).

$$ \text{EFF\%} \;=\; \left(1 + \frac{r_{\text{nominal}}}{m}\right)^{m} \;-\; 1 $$
r_nominalQuoted nominal annual rate (e.g., 0.13) mNumber of compounding periods per year (semi-annual = 2, monthly = 12, etc.)
EFF% for the 7.137% YTC above
Nominal YTC = 7.137% (= 2 × 3.568%) EFF% = (1 + 0.03568)² − 1 = 1.0726 − 1 = 0.0726 = 7.26%

So a "7.137% nominal" YTC is actually 7.26% effective. The two will diverge more as compounding becomes more frequent (monthly, daily). EFF% is what you'd use to fairly compare a semi-annual bond yield against, say, a monthly-compounded mortgage rate.

8 · Default risk, bond ratings, and what happens when a borrower can't pay

The bond formula assumed every coupon and the par value would actually get paid on time. In real life, sometimes the issuer runs out of money. That possibility — that the borrower fails to pay — is called default risk. This section is mostly definitional: what default is, how it's measured, and what happens to bondholders if it occurs.

8a · What is "default"?

Default = the issuer fails to meet a contractual obligation on the bond. Most commonly, this means missing a coupon payment or failing to repay par at maturity. Even a partial or late payment counts as default.

If a borrower defaults, the bondholder no longer receives the promised cash flows. The bond's value drops sharply, and the legal process for recovering some of the money begins (covered in 8d–8e below).

8b · Bond ratings — outsourced default-risk assessment

Two big rating agencies — Moody's and Standard & Poor's (S&P) — analyze each bond issuer and publish a letter grade summarizing the issuer's likelihood of paying back. Investors don't have to do their own credit analysis; they just check the rating.

Ratings are split into two big tiers:

TierMoody's gradesS&P gradesWhat it means
Investment gradeAaa, Aa, A, BaaAAA, AA, A, BBBConsidered safe enough for pension funds, banks, and other regulated institutions to hold. Low default risk.
Junk / SpeculativeBa, B, Caa, CBB, B, CCC, DHigher default risk → must offer higher yields to attract buyers. Many institutions are not allowed to hold these.

The dividing line: Baa / BBB is the lowest investment-grade rating. One notch below that (Ba / BB) is the highest junk rating. Crossing that line in either direction is a big deal — it changes who can legally own the bond.

8c · What goes into a rating? Three buckets of factors

The rating agencies look at three kinds of evidence:

Financial ratios
  • Debt ratio = total debt ÷ total assets. How leveraged is the company? More debt = more default risk.
  • TIE = "Times Interest Earned" = EBIT ÷ interest expense. How many times over can the company cover its interest bill from operating profit? Higher = safer.
  • FCC = "Fixed Charge Coverage" — same idea as TIE but includes lease payments and other fixed obligations, not just interest.
  • Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. Can the company pay its short-term bills? >1 means yes.
Bond-contract terms
  • Secured vs. unsecured — secured bonds are backed by specific collateral (e.g., a building); unsecured bonds aren't. Secured = safer.
  • Senior vs. subordinated — in a default, senior bondholders get paid before subordinated ones. Senior = safer.
  • Guarantees — a parent company or third party guarantees the debt.
  • Sinking fund (defined in §1j) — gradual repayment reduces risk.
  • Debt maturity — longer-dated debt has more time to go wrong.
Other / qualitative
  • Earnings stability — boring, steady cash flows are safer than volatile ones.
  • Regulatory environment — does the industry face price caps, sudden new rules?
  • Product liability — could a lawsuit wipe out the company?
  • Accounting policies — aggressive accounting hides problems.

8d · What happens when a borrower really can't pay — bankruptcy cancelled — not on the exam

If a company truly can't cover its debts, U.S. federal law gives it two main options under the Bankruptcy Act:

Chapter 11 — ReorganizationChapter 7 — Liquidation
What happensCompany keeps operating; existing debts get restructured (lower amounts, longer terms, etc.)Company shuts down; assets are sold off and the cash distributed to creditors
Who usually prefers itThe company itself (so management can stay in control and salvage the business)Sometimes secured creditors (they want their cash now)
Who runs itExisting management, supervised by a court-appointed trustee (an independent overseer) and the bankruptcy judgeThe trustee — management is gone

Chapter 11 process:

  1. Company files a Ch.11 petition. This immediately stops creditors from foreclosing or seizing assets ("automatic stay").
  2. Within 120 days, the company submits a reorganization plan — a proposal for new debt amounts, payment schedules, and equity arrangements.
  3. The plan must show the company is "worth more alive than dead" — i.e., creditors will recover more under reorganization than they would in liquidation.
  4. Creditor groups vote on the plan. If a majority of creditors and the judge approve, the company emerges from bankruptcy with reduced debts and a fresh start.
  5. If the plan fails, the judge orders a conversion to Chapter 7 — the company gets liquidated.

8e · Liquidation payoff order — exam favorite (STILL IN SCOPE)

If a company is liquidated under Chapter 7, the cash from selling the assets is paid out in this strict legal order. Each tier must be fully paid before the next tier gets anything. Memorize this list — it shows up on multiple-choice questions almost every year.

  1. Secured creditors — get paid from the sale of the specific assets that secured their loans (e.g., the bondholder of a mortgage bond gets the proceeds from selling the building).
  2. Trustee's costs — the bankruptcy administrator's fees.
  3. Wages owed to employees — subject to legal caps.
  4. Taxes — owed to government agencies.
  5. Unfunded pension liabilities — owed to retirees.
  6. Unsecured creditors — anyone who lent money without collateral, including unsecured bondholders.
  7. Preferred stockholders — equity holders with priority over common.
  8. Common stockholders — almost always get $0.

Notice unsecured creditors are sixth in line. By the time the assets reach them, there's usually nothing left. That's why unsecured bondholders typically get pennies on the dollar in a Ch.7 — and why they often agree to substantial debt write-downs in Ch.11 negotiations: a 40% recovery in reorganization beats a 0% recovery in liquidation.

9 · Exam traps (pulled from past finals)

These are the question patterns that actually appear on the final. Recognize them before the exam, not during.

  1. Premium ↔ coupon vs YTM direction. "If YTM > coupon, the bond sells at a premium" — FALSE. YTM > coupon ⇒ discount. (Easy reverse-trap.)
  2. Call provision ownership. "Call provision gives bondholders the right to demand early repayment" — FALSE. Issuers, not bondholders.
  3. Reinvestment vs price-risk swap. "All else equal, high-coupon bonds have more reinvestment risk than low-coupon bonds" — TRUE. Big coupons = big cash flows to redeploy.
  4. Maturity → price sensitivity. "For a given Δkd, the longer the maturity, the larger the change in price" — TRUE. (Sometimes phrased with two blanks: longer → larger.)
  5. Semi-annual conversion. If a question says "semi-annual" or "every 6 months", remember to double N, halve kd, halve coupon. The most common mistake is forgetting one of the three.
  6. Callable premium → use YTC, not YTM. If the bond sells at a premium and is callable, the relevant yield is YTC. FV = call price (not par), N = periods to call (not maturity).
  7. cancelledEffective vs nominal yield. EFF% = (1 + r/m)m − 1. Used to compare a bond's yield against a different compounding frequency (e.g., a monthly mortgage).
  8. "True or False — all 10-year bonds have the same risk." FALSE. Coupon rate also matters: low-coupon = more price risk, less reinvestment risk.

1 · What is a stock? (assume you know nothing)

A reminder of how Ch 8 framed bonds: a bond is a written IOU — a loan with fixed promised payments. A stock is the opposite kind of investment: not a loan, but a piece of ownership.

Imagine your friend wants to open a bakery. Instead of borrowing $100,000 at 10% interest (which would be a bond), she sells 100 small "slices" of the bakery for $1,000 each. Whoever buys a slice owns 1% of the bakery — including 1% of any future profits. That slice is a share of stock.

Stocks (also called shares or equity) represent ownership of a company. Owning one share of a company that has issued one million shares means you own one millionth of that company. Bonds make you a lender; stocks make you a part-owner.

1a · The cash a stockholder actually receives

A bondholder gets two clear cash streams (coupons + par). A stockholder gets a much messier picture:

  • Dividends — periodic cash payments the company chooses to send out to shareholders from its profits (typically every quarter). Companies are NOT obligated to pay dividends. If the company has no profits or wants to reinvest, it can skip dividends with no legal consequence — unlike a missed bond coupon, which would be default. Symbol: D with a subscript indicating the year (D₁ = next year's dividend, D₂ = year-2 dividend, etc.).
  • Capital gain — the increase in the share price between when you buy and when you sell. If you buy at $50 and sell at $60, that's a $10 capital gain per share. This is profit only when you actually sell.

Total stockholder return = dividends collected + capital gain (or loss). No fixed promise, no maturity date, no par value to redeem at — the share keeps existing as long as the company exists.

1b · Vocabulary you'll see throughout the chapter

a · Common stock

The default flavor of stock — the one most people mean when they just say "stock." Holders have voting rights (1 share = 1 vote, typically), share in profits via dividends, and elect the board of directors. Last in line if the company goes bankrupt (recall §8e from Ch 8).

b · Preferred stock (a hybrid)

A second class of stock that sits between bonds and common stock. Pays a fixed dividend (like a bond's coupon) that gets paid before any common-stock dividend. But unlike a bond's interest, a missed preferred dividend doesn't trigger bankruptcy — the company is allowed to skip it. Covered fully in §10.

c · Classified stock

When a company creates multiple categories of common stock with different voting/dividend rules. Example: founders' shares = full voting rights but limited dividends; Class A shares = limited voting but full dividends. Companies use this to let founders keep control while raising money from outside investors.

d · Initial Public Offering (IPO)

The very first time a company sells stock to the general public. Before an IPO the company is private (only owned by founders, employees, and a few private investors); after the IPO it is public and anyone can buy shares on a stock exchange. The phrase "going public" = doing an IPO.

e · ks — required rate of return on a stock

Same idea as k_d from Ch 8, but for stocks. ks is the return investors demand to be willing to hold this stock today, given its risk level. The "s" stands for "stock."

Where does ks come from? From the CAPM / SML equation (covered in detail in Ch 5, but the formula is ks = kRF + (kM − kRF)·β — explained in §3 below when we first need it).

f · g — the dividend growth rate

How fast the company's dividends are expected to grow each year. If today's dividend is $2 and g = 6%, next year's dividend is $2 × 1.06 = $2.12; the year after is $2.12 × 1.06 = $2.247; and so on. Symbol: g. Can be positive (growing dividends), zero (flat), or even negative (declining).

g · P₀ vs. P̂₀ ("P-zero" vs. "P-zero hat")

Notation alert. You'll see two slightly different symbols:

  • P₀ = the current actual market price you'd pay if you bought today.
  • P̂₀ (with a hat) = the theoretical intrinsic value calculated from a model. The "hat" means "estimated" or "expected."

If a stock is fairly priced, P₀ = P̂₀. If P̂₀ > P₀, the model thinks the stock is undervalued (buy!); if P̂₀ < P₀, overvalued (sell or avoid).

h · Three terms previewed for §6 (free cash flow method)
  • Free Cash Flow (FCF) = the cash a company generates from operations after paying for taxes and after investing in the equipment/buildings it needs to keep running. Roughly: profit you could pull out of the company without crippling it.
  • NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax = operating income × (1 − tax rate). It's earnings before interest expense, then taxed.
  • Terminal value (TV) = the lump-sum estimate of all cash flows beyond a forecasting horizon. Used when you can't model dividends or FCF forever, so you forecast for a few years explicitly and then collapse "everything after" into a single number using the constant-growth shortcut.

2 · Why does a stock have any value at all? (the dividend discount model)

Bonds are easy to value because they have contractually-promised cash flows (coupons + par). Stocks have no promised cash flows. So why would anyone pay $50 for a piece of paper that doesn't promise anything?

Answer: the only thing the stockholder will ever receive from the company is dividends — either now, or in the future, or eventually if the company is liquidated. So the fair value of a stock today is the present value of every dividend you'll ever collect from it.

2a · The dividend discount model (master formula for stocks)

Apply the same time-value-of-money logic from Ch 8 (§2a). Each future dividend D₁, D₂, D₃, … gets discounted back to today using the required rate ks:

$$ \hat{P}_0 \;=\; \frac{D_1}{(1+k_s)^1} \;+\; \frac{D_2}{(1+k_s)^2} \;+\; \frac{D_3}{(1+k_s)^3} \;+\; \cdots \;+\; \frac{D_\infty}{(1+k_s)^\infty} $$
P̂₀Theoretical stock price today (the model's estimate) D_tDollar dividend expected in year t k_sRequired rate of return on the stock (the discount rate) ∞Forever — the company is assumed to live indefinitely, so the sum runs to infinity

Read it: "the stock is worth the present value of every dividend the company will ever pay, discounted at the rate I demand for taking on the stock's risk." This is the Dividend Discount Model (DDM). It's the foundation of every other formula in this chapter.

Why "what about capital gains"? Doesn't a stockholder also benefit from selling at a higher price? Yes — but the next person who buys it pays based on their expected future dividends. Trace the chain back and you find: ultimately, every owner along the chain receives only dividends. The capital gain you collect is just the discount adjustment as future dividends move closer in time. So PV-of-dividends already captures everything.

Practical problem with the DDM: nobody can forecast every dividend out to infinity. To make the formula usable, we make simplifying assumptions about how dividends grow. That gives us §3 (constant growth), §4 (zero growth), and §5 (supernormal growth).

3 · The constant-growth (Gordon) model — the workhorse formula

3a · The simplifying assumption

Suppose dividends grow at a single constant rate g, forever. So:

$$ D_1 = D_0(1+g)^1, \quad D_2 = D_0(1+g)^2, \quad D_t = D_0(1+g)^t $$
D_0The dividend the company JUST paid (today, time = 0) D_1The dividend expected NEXT year gThe constant annual growth rate (e.g., 0.06 for 6%)

Plug those into the DDM and you get an infinite series. With a bit of algebra (the geometric-series trick), the infinite sum collapses to a single tiny formula:

3b · The Gordon constant-growth formula

$$ \boxed{\;\hat{P}_0 \;=\; \frac{D_1}{k_s - g}\;} \qquad \text{equivalently:} \quad \hat{P}_0 = \frac{D_0(1+g)}{k_s - g} $$

"Next year's dividend, divided by (required return minus growth rate)." Named after economist Myron Gordon. This is the most-used formula in the chapter — memorize it.

Critical condition: the formula only works if ks > g. If g ≥ ks, the math gives a negative or undefined price (nonsense). Intuitively: if a company's dividends grew faster than your required return forever, its present value would be infinite — that can't be a real situation. So we always need ks > g, AND g must be expected to stay constant forever.
Stock price P̂₀ as g approaches ks = 13% (D₀ = $2 fixed). Notice the curve explodes upward as g gets close to 13% — a small change in g near the boundary causes huge price swings. This is why "g constant forever, slightly less than ks" is a strong assumption: the model is extremely sensitive in this region. Beyond g = 13%, the formula breaks (negative denominator).

3c · How to find ks — the CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model)

Where does ks come from? From the Security Market Line (SML) equation, the central result of the CAPM:

$$ k_s \;=\; k_{RF} \;+\; (k_M - k_{RF}) \cdot \beta_{\text{firm}} $$
k_RFRisk-free rate — the return on a Treasury bill (a short-term debt security issued by the U.S. government, considered default-free because the government can always print dollars to repay) k_MExpected return on the overall stock market (e.g., the S&P 500 — an index of the 500 largest U.S. public companies, used as a stand-in for "the market" as a whole) k_M − k_RFMarket risk premium — the extra return investors demand for holding stocks instead of T-bills β (beta)How sensitive THIS stock is to overall market moves. β = 1 means moves with the market; β > 1 means more volatile; β < 1 means less volatile

CAPM is covered in detail in Ch 5. For Ch 9 problems, you'll typically be given β, kRF, and kM and just plug them into the SML formula.

Worked example · Find the required return ks

Given: β = 1.2, kRF = 7%, kM = 12%.

k_s = 7% + (12% − 7%) × 1.2 = 7% + 5% × 1.2 = 7% + 6% = 13%

So investors require a 13% return on this stock.

3d · Worked example · The full constant-growth pricing

Same firm. The dividend just paid was D₀ = $2.00, and dividends are expected to grow at g = 6% forever. We just computed ks = 13%. Find the stock's intrinsic value P̂₀.

Step-by-step
D_0 = $2.00, g = 6%, k_s = 13% Step 1 — find next year's dividend: D_1 = D_0 × (1 + g) = 2.00 × 1.06 = $2.12 Step 2 — apply Gordon formula: P̂_0 = D_1 / (k_s − g) = 2.12 / (0.13 − 0.06) = 2.12 / 0.07 = $30.29

So the stock should trade at $30.29 today. If the actual market price is $30.29, the stock is fairly priced. If it's $25, the model thinks it's underpriced; at $35 the model thinks it's overpriced.

3e · Price one year from now

Same logic, just shift the timeline forward by one year. After D₁ has been paid, the future dividend stream becomes D₂, D₃, D₄, … so:

$$ \hat{P}_1 \;=\; \frac{D_2}{k_s - g} $$

Or even simpler: in a constant-growth world, the price grows at the same rate g. So P̂₁ = P̂₀ × (1+g).

Same example — price at end of year 1
D_2 = D_1 × (1 + g) = 2.12 × 1.06 = $2.247 Method A: P̂_1 = D_2 / (k_s − g) = 2.247 / 0.07 = $32.10 Method B: P̂_1 = P̂_0 × (1 + g) = 30.29 × 1.06 = $32.10 ✓

Both methods give the same answer. Use whichever is faster.

3f · Decomposing total return — dividend yield + capital gains yield

Just like YTM = current yield + capital gains yield for bonds (Ch 8 §4e), a stock's total return splits into two pieces:

$$ \text{Dividend yield} \;=\; \frac{D_1}{P_0} \qquad \text{Capital gains yield} \;=\; \frac{P_1 - P_0}{P_0} \;=\; g $$ $$ \boxed{\;k_s \;=\; \frac{D_1}{P_0} \;+\; g\;} $$

In a constant-growth world, the capital gains yield equals the dividend growth rate g exactly. So ks splits cleanly into "the cash income you collect" plus "the price appreciation."

Decompose the 13% required return for our example
Dividend yield = D_1 / P_0 = 2.12 / 30.29 = 0.0700 = 7.0% Cap-gains yield = g = 6.0% Total return = 7.0% + 6.0% = 13.0% ✓ matches k_s

3g · Solving the formula backwards — finding the implied ks

Sometimes a problem gives you the actual market price and asks for the return investors must be expecting. Just rearrange:

$$ k_s \;=\; \frac{D_1}{P_0} \;+\; g $$

Same identity from §3f, used as a solver. If the market price is $30.29, D₁ = $2.12, and g = 6%, then the market is implicitly pricing in a 13% return.

4 · Special case · Zero growth (g = 0)

If a company pays the same dividend every year forever (no growth at all), the dividend stream becomes a perpetuity — an annuity that runs forever. Plug g = 0 into the Gordon formula:

$$ \hat{P}_0 \;=\; \frac{D_1}{k_s - 0} \;=\; \frac{D}{k_s} $$

Perpetuity formula: a constant cash flow forever, divided by the discount rate, equals its present value today.

Example · Perpetual $2 dividend at ks = 13%
D = $2.00 every year forever k_s = 13% P̂_0 = D / k_s = 2.00 / 0.13 = $15.38

The same dividend ($2) is worth a lot less ($15.38) when there's no growth than when it's growing at 6% ($30.29) — because growing dividends produce ever-larger future cash flows.

Dividend yield in the zero-growth case = D / P₀ = ks exactly (since cap-gains yield = g = 0). All return comes from dividends.

4b · Edge case · Negative growth (g < 0, declining dividends)

What if a company's dividends are shrinking each year (g is negative)? Surprisingly, the same Gordon formula still works, as long as ks > g (which is automatic if g is negative and ks is positive). The stock just ends up with a low value because dividends are dying off.

Example · D₀ = $2, g = −6%, ks = 13%
D_1 = D_0 × (1 + g) = 2.00 × (1 − 0.06) = 2.00 × 0.94 = $1.88 P̂_0 = D_1 / (k_s − g) = 1.88 / (0.13 − (−0.06)) = 1.88 / 0.19 = $9.89

Anyone would still buy this stock — at the right (low) price — because the dividends are still positive, just shrinking. Decomposition: cap-gains yield = g = −6% (price drifting down); dividend yield = 13% − (−6%) = 19% (high enough to compensate for the price decay).

5 · Supernormal (non-constant) growth — the messy case

Real companies often grow much faster than 6%/year for a few years (think of a young tech company), then settle into a lower, sustainable rate as they mature. The Gordon formula by itself can't handle this — its assumption is constant growth forever — but we can combine it with manual discounting to handle the early "fast" years.

5a · The 4-step procedure

  1. Forecast each individual dividend during the supernormal period using the high g rate.
  2. At the end of the supernormal period (call it year N), apply the Gordon formula using the new, lower constant g to get a single price P̂N — the value at year N of all dividends from year N+1 onward.
  3. Discount everything back to today: each individual supernormal dividend AND the lump-sum P̂N, all using ks.
  4. Sum the discounted values. That's P̂₀.

5b · Worked example · 30% growth for 3 years, then 6% forever

Given: D₀ = $2.00. Dividends grow at g₁ = 30% for 3 years (years 1, 2, 3). Starting in year 4, growth slows to g₂ = 6% forever. ks = 13%.

Step 1 · Forecast each dividend
D_1 = 2.000 × 1.30 = $2.600 D_2 = 2.600 × 1.30 = $3.380 D_3 = 3.380 × 1.30 = $4.394 D_4 = 4.394 × 1.06 = $4.658 (first year of constant growth)
Step 2 · Apply Gordon formula at end of year 3 to get P̂₃

From year 4 onward, growth is constant at 6%. So at the end of year 3 we can use Gordon with D₄:

P̂_3 = D_4 / (k_s − g_2) = 4.658 / (0.13 − 0.06) = 4.658 / 0.07 = $66.54

$66.54 is the value at year 3 of every dividend from year 4 forward. We will discount this lump sum back to today.

Step 3 · Discount each piece back to today
PV(D_1) = 2.600 / 1.13^1 = 2.600 / 1.130 = $2.301 PV(D_2) = 3.380 / 1.13^2 = 3.380 / 1.277 = $2.647 PV(D_3) = 4.394 / 1.13^3 = 4.394 / 1.443 = $3.045 PV(P̂_3) = 66.54 / 1.13^3 = 66.54 / 1.443 = $46.116
Step 4 · Sum
P̂_0 = 2.301 + 2.647 + 3.045 + 46.116 = $54.109

So the stock's intrinsic value today is $54.11. Notice that most of the value (~$46 out of $54) comes from the terminal piece — the Gordon-formula chunk representing all dividends from year 4 onward. The 3 supernormal years contribute only ~$8 in PV terms.

Where the $54.11 actually comes from. The terminal value (PV of all dividends from year 4 forward) accounts for ~85% of the stock's worth. The 3 supernormal years contribute the remaining ~15%. Practical implication: in real-world DCF valuations, the terminal-value assumption is usually the most important number — small changes in long-run g flow into big changes in the answer.

5c · Yields during the supernormal period

One subtle point: during the supernormal years, the dividend yield and capital gains yield are NOT constant, and the capital gains yield is generally less than g.

Example, at t = 0 for our problem:

Dividend yield (t=0) = D_1 / P̂_0 = 2.60 / 54.11 = 4.81% Cap-gains yield (t=0) = k_s − div yield = 13.00% − 4.81% = 8.19% (Note: cap-gains yield 8.19% < g 30%. The two only equate once growth becomes constant.)

Once we're past year 3 and into the constant-growth regime, the relationship from §3f is restored: cap-gains yield = g = 6%, dividend yield = 7%, totaling ks = 13%.

5d · Zero-then-constant variant

Same recipe handles a different timeline: zero growth for 3 years, then 6% constant. D₀ = D₁ = D₂ = D₃ = $2.00, then D₄ = 2.12.

Worked numbers
PV(D_1) = 2.00 / 1.13^1 = $1.770 PV(D_2) = 2.00 / 1.13^2 = $1.566 PV(D_3) = 2.00 / 1.13^3 = $1.386 P̂_3 = D_4 / (k_s − g) = 2.12 / 0.07 = $30.29 PV(P̂_3) = 30.29 / 1.13^3 = $20.99 P̂_0 = 1.77 + 1.57 + 1.39 + 20.99 = $25.72 Yields at t = 0: Div yield = D_1 / P̂_0 = 2.00 / 25.72 = 7.78% Cap-gains = k_s − 7.78% = 5.22%

6 · Free Cash Flow (FCF) method — for companies with no dividends

The DDM works only if you can forecast dividends. That's a problem for the many companies that pay no dividends (especially young growth companies that reinvest everything). For those, we use the Free Cash Flow method instead.

6a · The big idea

Same time-value logic, but applied to the value of the entire firm instead of just the equity. The procedure:

  1. Forecast Free Cash Flow (FCF) for the next several years using FCF = NOPAT − Net capital investment. (NOPAT is operating profit after tax — defined in §1h.)
  2. Estimate a terminal value at the horizon using the Gordon trick (constant growth in FCF, discounted at the firm's overall cost of capital).
  3. Discount everything to today at the firm's overall cost of capital. Sum gives total firm value.
  4. Subtract the value of debt + preferred stock to get the value of common equity (market capitalization).
  5. Divide by number of shares outstanding to get the per-share price.

6b · Why this works

The FCF is the cash actually available to all the firm's investors combined (bondholders, preferred holders, common stockholders). PV-of-FCF gives the total firm value. Subtracting the bondholders' and preferred holders' claims leaves what's left for common stockholders. Same logic as DDM, just one level higher in the capital stack.

6c · Worked example

Given: FCF for the next 3 years is forecast at −$5M, +$10M, +$20M. Beyond year 3, FCF is expected to grow at 6%/year. The firm's overall cost of capital is 10%. (Cost of capital = the blended required return that the firm pays its mix of investors — bondholders + preferred + common — weighted by how much money each group provides. Formally called WACC = Weighted Average Cost of Capital. It plays the same role for the whole firm that ks plays for the equity alone.) The firm has $40M in debt and 10 million common shares.

Step 1 · Compute the terminal value at end of year 3
FCF_4 = FCF_3 × (1 + g) = 20 × 1.06 = $21.20M TV_3 = FCF_4 / (k − g) = 21.20 / (0.10 − 0.06) = 21.20 / 0.04 = $530M

$530M is the year-3 value of all FCF from year 4 onward.

Step 2 · Discount each piece to today
PV(FCF_1) = −5 / 1.10^1 = −$4.545M PV(FCF_2) = 10 / 1.10^2 = +$8.264M PV(FCF_3) = 20 / 1.10^3 = +$15.026M PV(TV_3) = 530 / 1.10^3 = +$398.197M Total firm value = sum = −4.545 + 8.264 + 15.026 + 398.197 = $416.94M
Step 3 · Convert firm value to per-share stock price
Equity value = Firm value − Debt = 416.94 − 40 = $376.94M Price per share = Equity value / Shares = 376.94 / 10 = $37.69

Note again: the terminal value dominates ($398M out of $417M total). This is typical and a key sensitivity to test: small changes in assumed long-run g flow into big changes in TV and therefore the stock price.

7 · Multiples — the back-of-envelope shortcut

The DDM and FCF methods are precise but require lots of forecasts. Analysts often use a faster shortcut: look at how comparable firms are priced, then scale that pricing to the company at hand. This is called relative valuation or using multiples.

7a · Common multiples

MultipleWhat it meansWhen it's used
P/E (price-to-earnings)Price per share ÷ earnings per share. "How many dollars are investors paying per $1 of annual earnings?"Most common. Used for profitable, mature companies.
P/CF (price-to-cash-flow)Price ÷ operating cash flow per shareBetter than P/E when accounting earnings are distorted (lots of non-cash items).
P/SalesPrice ÷ revenue per shareUsed for unprofitable companies (no E to ratio against). Common for early-stage growth firms.
P/CustomerMarket cap ÷ number of customersUsed for subscription businesses. Common in telecom, streaming, SaaS.

7b · How to use a multiple

  1. Find the average P/E (or other multiple) of comparable firms in the same industry.
  2. Multiply that average by the target company's expected earnings (or cash flow, etc.) per share.
  3. The result is your estimated stock price.

Example: if peer companies trade at average P/E = 15 and your company expects to earn $2.50/share next year, the multiples-based estimate is 15 × $2.50 = $37.50/share.

Multiples are quick and intuitive but assume the comparison firms are correctly priced. If the whole industry is in a bubble, multiples-based valuations will inflate too.

8 · Market equilibrium and what makes prices change

8a · What is "equilibrium"?

A stock is in market equilibrium when there's no general pressure for the price to move — buyers and sellers roughly balance each other. Mathematically, this means the expected return (what investors think they'll earn) equals the required return (what they demand to be willing to hold the stock):

$$ \underbrace{\frac{D_1}{P_0} + g}_{\text{expected }\hat{k}_s} \;\;=\;\; \underbrace{k_{RF} + (k_M - k_{RF})\beta}_{\text{required }k_s} $$

Left side comes from the dividend discount model (§3f); right side comes from CAPM/SML (§3c). When they're equal, the stock is fairly priced and there's no reason to buy or sell.

8b · How equilibrium gets restored when it's broken

Suppose a stock is mispriced — say, P₀ is "too low" relative to its fundamentals. Then:

  • Expected return = D₁/P₀ + g is high (small denominator → big yield).
  • That's higher than the required return → smart investors notice → buy orders > sell orders.
  • Demand pushes P₀ up.
  • As P₀ rises, D₁/P₀ falls back down toward the required return.
  • When they re-equalize, equilibrium is restored — no more excess buying.

The same mechanism in reverse pushes overpriced stocks down. Equilibrium is the natural resting point the market continually drifts toward as new information is processed.

8c · Why do stock prices change?

Look at the equilibrium price formula P̂₀ = D₁ / (ks − g). A change in any input changes the price:

  1. ks changes — the required return moves, e.g., because:
    • The risk-free rate (kRF) moves (Fed action, inflation expectations).
    • The market risk premium (kM − kRF) moves (general appetite for risk).
    • The firm's β changes (the company becomes riskier or safer).
  2. g changes — because of new economic data, new product launches, lawsuits, layoffs, anything that changes the expected growth path of dividends.

Both of these happen constantly as new information arrives — which is why prices jump around all day, every day.

9 · The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) cancelled — not on the exam

The Efficient Market Hypothesis is the academic finance theory that "securities prices already reflect all relevant information," so on average you can't pick stocks that beat the market — only luck or genuinely new information can give you an edge.

EMH comes in three flavors, each stronger than the previous one:

FormWhat information is "in" the price?What this impliesEmpirical verdict
Weak-form All past price data (charts, trends, historical patterns). You can't beat the market by looking at price charts alone. A recent drop tells you nothing about whether the stock will go up or down next. Mostly supported by evidence — though "technical analysis" (chart-reading) is still widely used in practice.
Semi-strong-form All publicly available info: past prices + financial statements + news + analyst reports. You can't beat the market by reading 10-K filings (the detailed annual financial report every U.S. public company must file with the SEC) or news either — by the time you've digested it, the price has already adjusted. Largely true, but superior analysts can occasionally profit by spotting and acting on new public information faster.
Strong-form All info, including insider (non-public) information. Even insiders couldn't make excess returns. NOT true. Insiders demonstrably can profit by trading on private info — which is why insider trading is illegal.

Why are markets typically efficient?

  1. ~15,000 trained analysts (MBAs, CFAs, technical PhDs) cover U.S. stocks full-time.
  2. They work for big firms (Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley, Prudential, hedge funds) with massive capital to put behind their views.
  3. They all have similar access to data via Bloomberg / Reuters / regulatory filings.
  4. So new news gets reflected in P₀ almost instantly — anyone slower than that is too late to profit.
Connection to valuation: if you believe in semi-strong EMH, then the market price IS the correct valuation. The DDM and FCF models are useful for understanding why the price is what it is, but not for finding underpriced stocks (because there aren't any). If you don't believe in EMH (i.e., you think the market is sometimes wrong), then the models are the way to find mispricings.

10b · Valuation formula

Most preferred stock pays a fixed dividend forever (perpetual). So preferred stock is a perpetuity, valued exactly like the §4 zero-growth common stock:

$$ V_p \;=\; \frac{D_p}{k_p} $$
V_pPreferred stock price D_pAnnual preferred dividend (fixed) k_pRequired rate of return on preferred stock

To find the expected return on a preferred stock given its market price, just rearrange:

$$ \hat{k}_p \;=\; \frac{D_p}{V_p} $$
Worked example · Dp = $5, Vp = $50
k̂_p = D_p / V_p = 5 / 50 = 0.10 = 10%

The preferred stock yields 10%. Since preferred dividends are fixed, this rate is roughly comparable to a bond's coupon yield in spirit, but with less default protection.

11 · Exam traps (pulled from past finals)

Patterns that actually appear on the exam. Recognize them now, not during.

  1. "If g = 5% constant, then dividend yield = 5%." → FALSE. Dividend yield = D₁/P₀ ≠ g (in general). What is 5% in a constant-growth world is the capital gains yield. Common reverse trap.
  2. "If g = 5%, the stock price is expected to be 5% higher next year." → TRUE. Constant-growth implies P̂₁ = P̂₀ × (1 + g). This is also why g = capital-gains yield.
  3. "The expected total return = 5% (= g)." → FALSE. Total return = dividend yield + g, both pieces matter.
  4. Find g from the price formula. Given P₀, D₁, and ks, find g. Use g = ks − D₁/P₀. Easy points if you remember the rearrangement.
  5. ks > g requirement. If a problem hands you g ≥ ks, the constant-growth model doesn't apply — must use a different method (likely supernormal growth followed by a constant phase).
  6. Expected return > required return → buy. If the stock is expected to deliver more than what you require, it's underpriced. Other variants ("…sell," "…growth supernormal," "…not maximizing price") are distractors.
  7. "Common stock value = PV of future dividends." → TRUE. Other choices ("PV of capital gains," "future value of dividends") are wrong on either the cash-flow type or the time direction.
  8. Combined CAPM + Gordon problem. Given β, kRF, kM, D₁, g — the steps are: (i) compute ks via CAPM, (ii) plug into Gordon for P̂₀. Two formulas in sequence.
  9. Total return decomposition. Expected total return = D₁/P₀ + g. If P₀ is given and growth is constant, this is direct.
  10. Market equilibrium definition. Equilibrium = "no incentive for general buying or selling" / "expected return = required return." Alternatives ("only in fiction," "prices fixed for the period") are distractors.

1 · What is an option? (assume you know nothing)

Bonds = a loan. Stocks = ownership. Options = neither. An option is a contract that gives you the right to buy or sell something later, at a price agreed today, but you don't have to. You only act if it's profitable for you.

Imagine someone tells you: "For $3 today, I'll let you decide three months from now whether to buy my $50 watch for $50. If the watch is worth $80 by then, you can pay me $50 and walk away with an $80 watch. If the watch is worth $20, just throw the contract away — you don't have to do anything."

You paid $3 for the right to buy at $50, but you have no obligation to do so. That contract is an option.

An option's value comes from this asymmetry: you get all the upside if things go your way, but you can walk away (losing only the small upfront fee) if they don't. That fee — what you paid for the contract — is called the option premium.

1a · Two kinds of options — calls and puts

Quick vocabulary: "bullish" = expects the price to go UP (named after a bull, which thrusts its horns upward when it attacks). "bearish" = expects the price to go DOWN (named after a bear, which swipes its paws downward). You'll see these terms throughout the chapter to describe what the option holder is betting on.

TypeGives the holder the right to…Profitable when…Holder expects price to…
Call option BUY the underlying asset at the strike price Market price > strike (so buying cheap and selling at the higher market price is profitable) RISE
Put option SELL the underlying asset at the strike price Market price < strike (so selling high to the writer and the writer paying more than market is profitable) FALL

Memory aid: Call = "call away the asset" → buy it. Put = "put it on someone" → sell it.

1b · Two parties to every option contract

  • Holder / Buyer / Long position — pays the premium upfront. Owns the right (not obligation) to exercise. Maximum loss = the premium paid.
  • Writer / Seller / Short position — collects the premium upfront. Has the obligation to honor the contract if the buyer chooses to exercise. Maximum profit = the premium received. The writer does NOT have the right to exercise — only the buyer does. (Common exam trap.)
Key asymmetry: the buyer's losses are capped (premium only) but gains can be unlimited. The writer's gains are capped (premium only) but losses can be unlimited (for a call writer) or very large (for a put writer). It's a transfer of risk from buyer to writer in exchange for the upfront premium payment.

1c · You can be long OR short in either calls OR puts → 4 possible positions

Long callBought a call (paid premium)Stock price to rise sharply
Short callWrote/sold a call (collected premium)Stock price to stay flat or fall (option expires worthless, you keep premium)

Short putWrote/sold a put (collected premium)Stock price to stay flat or rise (option expires worthless, you keep premium)

2 · Vocabulary you'll see throughout

a · Strike price (= Exercise price)

Definition: the price at which the underlying asset can be bought (call) or sold (put) when the option is exercised. Fixed in the contract. "Strike" and "exercise price" are perfect synonyms — interchangeable.

In our watch example: $50 was the strike price.

b · Expiration date

Definition: the last day the option contract is valid. After this day, the contract is dead — you can no longer exercise. Like a bond's maturity date.

Time to expiration = how long until expiration (e.g., 3 months, 1 year). The longer the time to expiration, the more "chances" the underlying price has to move favorably, so longer-dated options are worth more.

c · Option premium

Definition: the price you pay (or collect) for the option contract itself, paid upfront when the contract is created. This is NOT the same as the strike price. The premium is what changes hands today; the strike is the price that might change hands later if the option is exercised.

In our watch example: $3 was the premium.

The premium has two components, covered in §5: intrinsic value + time/speculative value.

d · Spot price (= Market price)

Definition: the current actual market price of the underlying asset (the stock, currency, etc.). "Spot" means "right now, on the spot." Often abbreviated as MP (market price) on the course tables.

e · Underlying asset

Definition: whatever the option is "on" — the thing the option grants you the right to buy or sell. Most often a stock, but can also be a currency, a bond, an index, or another option. The option's value depends on the underlying's price movements.

f · Exercise (verb)

Definition: to actually use the option's right to buy (call) or sell (put). Holders exercise when it's profitable; let the option expire when it's not.

Note: simply owning the option is NOT exercising. You must explicitly choose to exercise (and then you pay the strike, get the asset, etc.).

g · European vs. American options

Two styles, named for historical reasons (no real connection to actual continents):

  • European option — can only be exercised on the expiration date itself. No earlier.
  • American option — can be exercised any time up to and including the expiration date.

Consequence: the early-exercise flexibility of an American option has value (you don't lose anything by having the option to act early — only gain). So American options are worth more than otherwise-identical European options (or equal in special cases).

h · Where options are traded — context only
  • CBOE (Chicago Board Options Exchange) — exchange-traded equity options started here in 1972.
  • OTC (Over-the-Counter) — custom options that don't trade on a public exchange; arranged privately between two parties (banks, etc.). Goes back to the 1920s.
  • Philadelphia Stock Exchange — currency options started here in 1982.
  • CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange) — interest-rate options started here in 1985.

3 · Call options — full mechanics

3a · The call option's value at expiration

What is a call worth on the day it expires? Two cases:

  • If MP > Strike: the holder exercises — buys at strike (cheap) and could sell at market (high). Profit per share before premium = MP − Strike.
  • If MP ≤ Strike: the holder does NOT exercise (why pay strike for something you can buy in the market for less?). The call is worthless, value = $0.
$$ V_{\text{call}} \;=\; \max(\text{MP} - \text{Strike},\;\; 0) $$
V_callValue of the call option at expiration (also called intrinsic value) MPMarket (spot) price of the underlying at expiration StrikeStrike/exercise price written in the contract max(…, 0)"Whichever is greater" — guarantees the value never goes negative. The holder will simply not exercise when it would be a loss.

Important: the value V_call is before deducting the premium you paid upfront. To get your actual gain/loss as a buyer, subtract the premium: Buyer G/L = max(MP − Strike, 0) − Premium.

4 · Put options — full mechanics

4a · The put option's value at expiration

A put gives the right to sell, so the math flips:

  • If MP < Strike: the holder exercises — buys cheap in the market, sells at the high strike. Profit per share before premium = Strike − MP.
  • If MP ≥ Strike: holder does NOT exercise (why sell at strike when you can sell at market for more?). Value = $0.
$$ V_{\text{put}} \;=\; \max(\text{Strike} - \text{MP},\;\; 0) $$
V_putValue of the put option at expiration (intrinsic value) Strike − MPThe profit per share from exercising (compared to V_call which is MP − Strike)

Buyer G/L = Vput − Premium; Writer G/L = Premium − Vput. Same zero-sum logic as calls.

5 · Intrinsic value, speculative (time) value, and the option premium

5a · The two parts of every option premium

An option's premium (its market price before expiration) is split into two pieces:

$$ \boxed{\;\text{Option premium} \;=\; \text{Intrinsic value} \;+\; \text{Speculative (time) value}\;} $$
  • Intrinsic value = what the option would be worth if exercised right now. Same formulas as before:
    • Call intrinsic = max(MP − Strike, 0)
    • Put intrinsic = max(Strike − MP, 0)
    Always ≥ 0. Intrinsic value can never be negative because the holder simply wouldn't exercise.
  • Speculative value (= Time value) = the premium above intrinsic value. Captures the chance that the underlying could move in the holder's favor before expiration. Always ≥ 0 before expiration; falls to zero on the expiration date.

5b · What drives the time value? Five factors

From the slides, the time value of an option is determined by:

  1. Spot (underlying) price — how close it is to the strike.
  2. Volatility of the underlying — more volatile = more chance of a big favorable move = higher time value.
  3. Strike price — fixed in the contract; affects how far ITM or OTM the option is.
  4. Time to expiration — more time = more chance for the underlying to move = higher time value.
  5. Risk-free interest rate — the rate of interest you could earn on cash; affects the present value of the strike payment.

The slides phrase #5 as "the difference in the risk-free rate that can be earned by the two currencies" because the example was a currency option, but for stock options just read it as "the risk-free interest rate."

5c · Time decay — the most important behavior to remember

Time value falls toward zero as expiration approaches. On the expiration date itself, time value = 0 and the option's price equals exactly its intrinsic value. This phenomenon is called time decay (or "theta" in advanced courses). It works against option buyers: even if the stock doesn't move at all, the option you bought loses value every day.

An option whose premium equals its time value (i.e., intrinsic value = 0) is by definition out-of-the-money — see §6.

6 · Moneyness — In/At/Out of the Money

"Moneyness" describes the relationship between the underlying's spot price and the option's strike. Three buckets:

StatusFor a CALL optionFor a PUT optionIntrinsic value
In-the-money (ITM) Spot > Strike Spot < Strike Positive — exercising right now would yield cash
At-the-money (ATM) Spot = Strike Spot = Strike Exactly $0
Out-of-the-money (OTM) Spot < Strike Spot > Strike $0 — exercising would be a loss; holder won't exercise

The simple test: an option is ITM if exercising it right now would put cash in your pocket (before considering the premium you originally paid). It's OTM if exercising would lose money. ATM if breakeven.

Note: moneyness is about intrinsic value only, not the premium. An OTM option still has positive premium because it has time value (positive chance of moving ITM before expiration) — but its intrinsic value is $0.

7 · Exam traps (pulled from past finals)

Patterns that actually appear on the exam:

  1. "An option that allows the investor to BUY the stock under specified conditions is a ___." → Call. A put gives the right to sell. (Q30 on the spring 2023 final.)
  2. "The option WRITER has the right to exercise the option." → FALSE. Only the BUYER (holder) has the right to exercise. The writer has the obligation, not the right. (Q32 on the same final — common multiple-choice trap.)
  3. "When the market price of the stock is HIGHER than the exercise price, the buyer of the CALL option will achieve gains." → TRUE (assuming MP − Strike > Premium). Q33 on the final. Slight nuance: technically the buyer is "in the money" when MP > Strike but only profitable above breakeven (Strike + Premium). The exam answer accepts this as TRUE.
  4. "American options can be exercised only at expiration; European options can be exercised any time prior to expiration." → FALSE / FALSE. Reversed. American = anytime; European = only at expiration. (Q24 lists 4 choices; the correct one is "European options can be exercised only at expiration.")
  5. Call writer's outlook. "A call writer expects the stock to:" → remain the same or move down. If the stock stays flat or falls, the call expires worthless and the writer keeps the premium. The writer does NOT want the stock to rise. (Q21.)
  6. Breakeven formulas — memorize both: Call breakeven = Strike + Premium. Put breakeven = Strike − Premium. Easy to mix up under time pressure.
  7. Premium = intrinsic + time value. Used in conceptual questions like "if an option is OTM, its premium consists entirely of …" → time value (since intrinsic = 0).
  8. American > European in value. Never less. The early-exercise option has positive value (or zero in special cases).
  9. Maximum loss for a call buyer. Always = the premium paid. Not "unlimited" (that's the writer of a naked call).
  10. Zero-sum. Every dollar the buyer makes, the writer loses, and vice versa. If a question asks "if the buyer's profit is X, what is the writer's profit?" → answer is −X.

1 · Statistics primer (read this BEFORE any finance formula)

Every quantity in this chapter — expected return, risk, beta, alpha — is a statistics formula in finance clothing. If the statistics piece is unfamiliar, the finance derivation looks like nonsense. So we define each statistical concept here, in plain English, before using it.

1a · The big idea — uncertainty and probability distributions

The future is uncertain. We don't know whether next year a stock will return +20%, +5%, or −10%. But we can list possible outcomes and assign each one a probability (a number between 0 and 1, where 0 = impossible and 1 = certain). The full list of outcomes + probabilities is called a probability distribution.

Three ground rules for a valid probability distribution:

  1. Every probability is between 0 and 1 (or, equivalently, between 0% and 100%).
  2. The probabilities of all possible outcomes must add up to exactly 1 (or 100%) — because something must happen.
  3. Each "state" (outcome) is mutually exclusive — only one happens, but together they cover all possibilities.

Example we'll use throughout the chapter: the economy can be in one of 4 states next period (recession, slow growth, normal, boom). The probability and the return on Stock A in each state are:

1b · Expected value (also called "mean" or "expectation")

Definition: the probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes — what you'd expect to get "on average" if the experiment were repeated many times. Symbol: E[X] (read "E of X" or "expected value of X").

$$ E[X] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i \cdot x_i $$
x_iThe i-th possible outcome (a number, like 5%, 10%, etc.) p_iThe probability of that outcome (between 0 and 1) NNumber of possible outcomes ΣSigma: "add these up" (covered in Ch 8 §2c)

Why "weighted" matters: if outcomes have different probabilities, you can't just average them — you must weight each outcome by its probability. Outcomes that are very likely contribute a lot to the expected value; outcomes that are unlikely contribute very little.

Tiny example to anchor the concept: roll a fair 6-sided die. Outcomes are {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}, each with probability 1/6. E[outcome] = (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 3.5. Half the time you get higher, half lower. The expected value need not be a possible outcome (you'll never roll a 3.5).

1c · Variance (σ²) — measure of how much outcomes typically deviate from the mean

Definition: the probability-weighted average of the squared deviations from the mean. Symbol: σ² (Greek lowercase sigma, squared) or Var(X).

$$ \sigma^2 \;=\; \text{Var}(X) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i (x_i - E[X])^2 $$
(x_i − E[X])The deviation: how far this outcome is from the mean. Can be positive (above mean) or negative (below mean) (...)2The squared deviation. Always positive (negatives become positives) and penalizes large deviations more than small ones

Why square the deviations? Two reasons:

  1. Signs would cancel. Without squaring, positive and negative deviations would offset (large positives and large negatives would still average to zero). Squaring makes every deviation contribute positively.
  2. Big deviations punished disproportionately. A deviation of 4 contributes 16 to variance; a deviation of 2 contributes only 4. So variance heavily reflects extreme outcomes — useful for capturing risk.

Variance has weird units: if x is in %, variance is in %² (percent-squared), which is hard to interpret. That's why we usually take the square root → standard deviation.

1d · Standard deviation (σ) — variance in normal units

Definition: the positive square root of variance. Symbol: σ or SD(X).

$$ \sigma \;=\; \sqrt{\sigma^2} \;=\; \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)} $$

Standard deviation is in the same units as X (so if returns are in %, σ is in %). It's interpreted as "the typical deviation of an outcome from the mean." A return distribution with σ = 5% is much "tighter" around its mean than one with σ = 25%.

In finance: standard deviation of returns IS the most common measure of risk. Bigger σ = more uncertain outcomes = riskier.

1e · Covariance — how two random variables move together

Definition: a measure of whether two random variables tend to move together (both up, or both down) or in opposite directions.

$$ \text{Cov}(X,Y) \;=\; \sigma_{X,Y} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i (x_i - E[X])(y_i - E[Y]) $$
(x_i − E[X])(y_i − E[Y])The product of two deviations from their respective means. Sign of this product tells you the relationship in state i:
  • Both above means (both positive deviations) → product positive → contributes positively to covariance.
  • Both below means (both negative deviations) → product still positive → also contributes positively.
  • One above, one below → product negative → contributes negatively.

Interpretation of the sign:

  • Cov > 0 → variables tend to move together (positive relationship).
  • Cov < 0 → variables tend to move opposite (negative relationship).
  • Cov = 0 → no linear relationship.

Problem with covariance: the magnitude is hard to interpret because it depends on the units and scales of X and Y. So we usually convert it to a more interpretable number — the correlation coefficient.

1f · Correlation coefficient (ρ) — covariance, standardized to [−1, +1]

Definition: covariance divided by the product of the two standard deviations. Always falls between −1 and +1, regardless of units. Symbol: ρ (Greek rho, "row") or Corr(X,Y).

$$ \rho_{X,Y} \;=\; \text{Corr}(X,Y) \;=\; \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y} $$

Interpretation of values:

  • ρ = +1 → perfect positive correlation (variables move in lockstep, same direction, proportional magnitudes).
  • ρ = 0 → no linear relationship.
  • ρ = −1 → perfect negative correlation (variables move in lockstep, opposite directions).
  • 0 < ρ < 1 → positive, imperfect relationship.
  • −1 < ρ < 0 → negative, imperfect relationship.

Correlation captures the direction and strength of the linear relationship; covariance captures the direction and unit-scaled magnitude. They always agree on sign.

1g · Weighted average — used for portfolio returns

Definition: an average where each value is multiplied by a "weight" reflecting its importance. The weights must sum to 1.

$$ \text{Weighted average} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot x_i, \quad \text{where} \quad \sum w_i = 1 $$

In a portfolio, the weights wi are the fractions of total money invested in each asset. If you put $750 in Stock A and $250 in Stock B (total $1,000), then wA = 750/1000 = 0.75 and wB = 250/1000 = 0.25. They sum to 1.

(This same formula reappears throughout: expected return is a weighted average over states, portfolio return is a weighted average over assets.)

1h · Beta (β) — sensitivity of one variable to another

Definition: a measure of how much one variable moves in response to a 1-unit move in another. In finance, "beta" specifically means the sensitivity of a stock's return to the overall market's return.

$$ \beta_i \;=\; \frac{\text{Cov}(R_i, R_M)}{\text{Var}(R_M)} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_{i,M}}{\sigma_M^2} $$
R_iReturn on stock i R_MReturn on the overall market (e.g., the S&P 500 index)

Interpretation (memorize):

  • β = 1 → the stock moves exactly with the market. If the market is up 1%, the stock is expected to be up 1%.
  • β > 1 → the stock is more volatile than the market. If β = 1.5 and the market is up 1%, the stock is expected to be up 1.5%. More risk, more reward potential.
  • β < 1 (but positive) → the stock is less volatile than the market. β = 0.5 means it moves only half as much in either direction.
  • β = 0 → the stock's returns don't respond to market moves at all (rare; e.g., short-term Treasury bills).
  • β < 0 → the stock moves opposite to the market. Rare, but possible (gold mining can sometimes have small negative β).

Why beta matters in finance: when you hold a diversified portfolio, the only risk that "shows up" in your portfolio's variance is the part of each stock's return that moves with the market. Stock-specific quirks tend to cancel out across many holdings (covered in §6). So beta — the part that doesn't get diversified away — becomes the right risk measure for an individual stock inside a portfolio.

1i · Two important risk concepts (used in §6 and beyond)

  • Systematic risk = market risk = non-diversifiable risk. Affects all stocks (e.g., recession, inflation, interest rate hikes). Cannot be eliminated by holding many stocks. Beta measures this.
  • Unsystematic risk = company-specific risk = idiosyncratic risk = diversifiable risk. Specific to one firm (e.g., CEO scandal, factory fire, missed earnings). Can be largely eliminated by holding many stocks because the bad luck of one firm is offset by the good luck of others.

Total risk = Systematic + Unsystematic. Standard deviation captures total risk. Beta captures only the systematic piece.

2 · Expected return on a stock

Now we apply the statistics from §1 to the finance domain. The first concept: a stock's expected return, which is just the expected value of its return random variable.

$$ E[R] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i \cdot R_i $$

Same formula as §1b, just with R (return) in place of generic X. Read: "the probability-weighted average of all possible returns."

Worked example · Expected return for Stock A

Worked example · Expected return for Stock B
E[R_B] = 0.20 × 50% + 0.30 × 30% + 0.30 × 10% + 0.20 × (−10%) = 10% + 9% + 3% + (−2%) = 20%

So Stock B has a higher expected return than Stock A (20% vs. 12.5%). But that's only half the story — we haven't measured risk yet.

3 · Risk of a single stock — variance and standard deviation

Risk = the chance that the actual return differs from what we expected. Captured by variance and standard deviation, applied to the return distribution.

$$ \sigma^2 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i (R_i - E[R])^2 \qquad \sigma \;=\; \sqrt{\sigma^2} $$
Worked example · Variance & SD for Stock A

E[RA] = 12.5% = 0.125. Compute each squared deviation, weight by probability, sum:

σ²_A = 0.2 × (0.05 − 0.125)² + 0.3 × (0.10 − 0.125)² + 0.3 × (0.15 − 0.125)² + 0.2 × (0.20 − 0.125)² = 0.2 × 0.005625 + 0.3 × 0.000625 + 0.3 × 0.000625 + 0.2 × 0.005625 = 0.001125 + 0.0001875 + 0.0001875 + 0.001125 = 0.002625 σ_A = √0.002625 = 0.0512 = 5.12%

So Stock A's typical deviation is ~5.12% from its mean of 12.5%. A "tight" distribution.

Worked example · Variance & SD for Stock B
σ²_B = 0.2 × (0.50 − 0.20)² + 0.3 × (0.30 − 0.20)² + 0.3 × (0.10 − 0.20)² + 0.2 × (−0.10 − 0.20)² = 0.2 × 0.09 + 0.3 × 0.01 + 0.3 × 0.01 + 0.2 × 0.09 = 0.018 + 0.003 + 0.003 + 0.018 = 0.042 σ_B = √0.042 = 0.2049 = 20.49%

Stock B's typical deviation is 20.49% — about 4× wider than Stock A's. So Stock B is much riskier on a stand-alone basis.

4 · Portfolio expected return — weighted average of the parts

Most investors don't hold a single stock; they hold a portfolio (combination of multiple stocks). Step 1 of portfolio analysis: find the portfolio's expected return.

The portfolio expected return is the weighted average of the individual stocks' expected returns:

$$ E[R_p] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot E[R_i] $$
E[R_p]Expected return of the portfolio w_iFraction of portfolio money invested in stock i E[R_i]Expected return of stock i (computed individually as in §2)

Weights MUST sum to 1 (you must invest all the money somewhere). For a 2-stock portfolio:

$$ E[R_p] \;=\; w_1 E[R_1] \;+\; w_2 E[R_2] $$
Worked example · 50/50 portfolio of A and B
w_A = 0.50, w_B = 0.50 E[R_A] = 12.5%, E[R_B] = 20% E[R_p] = 0.50 × 12.5% + 0.50 × 20% = 6.25% + 10% = 16.25%
Important: portfolio expected return is always exactly the weighted average of individual expected returns. Never "more" or "less" — the math is rigid here. (This is a common exam trap. Q23 in past finals.)

The interesting fact about portfolios is what happens to risk, not return — covered next.

5 · Portfolio risk — covariance, correlation, and the variance formula

Portfolio risk is not a simple weighted average. It depends on how the stocks move together. Two stocks that always move in opposite directions can be combined into a portfolio with much lower risk than either stock alone — that's the magic of diversification.

5a · Covariance between two stocks

Apply the §1e formula to two stocks' returns:

$$ \text{Cov}(R_A, R_B) \;=\; \sigma_{A,B} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i (R_{A,i} - E[R_A])(R_{B,i} - E[R_B]) $$
Worked example · Cov(A, B)

E[RA] = 0.125, E[RB] = 0.20.

σ_AB = 0.2 × (0.05 − 0.125)(0.50 − 0.20) + 0.3 × (0.10 − 0.125)(0.30 − 0.20) + 0.3 × (0.15 − 0.125)(0.10 − 0.20) + 0.2 × (0.20 − 0.125)(−0.10 − 0.20) = 0.2 × (−0.075)(0.30) = −0.0045 + 0.3 × (−0.025)(0.10) = −0.00075 + 0.3 × (0.025)(−0.10) = −0.00075 + 0.2 × (0.075)(−0.30) = −0.0045 = −0.0105

The covariance is negative. Whenever Stock A's return is above its mean, Stock B's tends to be below its mean — and vice versa. They move in opposite directions.

5b · Correlation coefficient between two stocks

Standardize the covariance into a [−1, +1] number:

$$ \rho_{A,B} \;=\; \frac{\text{Cov}(R_A, R_B)}{\sigma_A \cdot \sigma_B} $$
Worked example · ρ(A, B)
ρ_AB = −0.0105 / (0.0512 × 0.2049) = −0.0105 / 0.01049 = −1.00

ρ = −1, the most extreme negative value. Stocks A and B are perfectly negatively correlated — they move in exactly opposite directions in every state. (This is engineered to make the example clean; real-world ρ is rarely exactly ±1.)

5c · Portfolio variance — the magic formula

For a 2-stock portfolio, the variance combines the individual variances and a covariance term:

$$ \sigma_p^2 \;=\; w_A^2 \sigma_A^2 \;+\; w_B^2 \sigma_B^2 \;+\; 2 w_A w_B \, \sigma_{A,B} $$

Or equivalently, using correlation:

$$ \sigma_p^2 \;=\; w_A^2 \sigma_A^2 \;+\; w_B^2 \sigma_B^2 \;+\; 2 w_A w_B \, \rho_{A,B} \, \sigma_A \sigma_B $$

The middle term is what matters. If ρ = +1, the term is positive and large — portfolio risk is just a weighted sum of individual risks (no diversification benefit). If ρ = 0, the term vanishes — partial diversification. If ρ = −1, the term is large negative — diversification cancels out almost all risk.

Worked example · 75% A + 25% B portfolio variance
w_A = 0.75, w_B = 0.25 σ_A = 0.0512, σ_B = 0.2049, ρ_AB = −1 σ²_p = (0.75)² × (0.0512)² + (0.25)² × (0.2049)² + 2(0.75)(0.25)(−1)(0.0512)(0.2049) = 0.5625 × 0.002625 + 0.0625 × 0.042 + 2 × 0.1875 × (−1)(0.01049) = 0.001477 + 0.002625 + (−0.003934) = 0.000168 ≈ 0.00016 σ_p = √0.00016 = 0.0128 = 1.28%
Look at this! The 75/25 portfolio has σ_p ≈ 1.28%. Compare to the standalone risks:
  • Stock A alone: σ = 5.12%
  • Stock B alone: σ = 20.49%
  • 75/25 portfolio: σ = 1.28% — far below either!
Combining a low-risk stock with a high-risk stock that moves in the opposite direction has produced a portfolio that's almost risk-free. This is the power of diversification. And the portfolio still earns: E[Rp] = 0.75 × 12.5% + 0.25 × 20% = 14.375% (better than Stock A alone).

6 · Diversification — what it can and can't eliminate

The 75/25 example showed that combining stocks reduces total risk. But there's a limit. Some risk can't be diversified away no matter how many stocks you hold.

6b · Random diversification

Even random stock-picking diversifies away unsystematic risk if you hold enough stocks (typically 20–30 is enough to eliminate most company-specific risk). It does NOT eliminate market risk — market risk affects all stocks together. So:

  • Random diversification reduces unsystematic (firm-specific) risk.
  • Random diversification does NOT reduce systematic (market) risk.
  • Adding more stocks doesn't increase a portfolio's expected return (just changes the mean).
  • Adding more stocks doesn't increase a portfolio's beta toward extreme values — random adding tends toward β ≈ 1 (the market average).

7 · Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)

If diversification can eliminate unsystematic risk, then the only risk that matters for an individual stock (held inside a diversified portfolio) is its systematic risk — i.e., its beta. The CAPM formalizes this insight as a pricing equation.

7a · The CAPM / Security Market Line (SML) equation

$$ \boxed{\;K_i \;=\; K_{RF} \;+\; \beta_i (K_M - K_{RF})\;} $$
K_iRequired return on stock i (the rate of return investors demand) K_RFRisk-free rate of return — the yield on a Treasury bill (short-term U.S. government debt, considered default-free) β_iBeta of stock i (its systematic-risk sensitivity) K_MExpected return on the market portfolio (K_M − K_RF)The market risk premium — the extra return demanded for holding "the market" instead of the risk-free asset. The reward for taking systematic risk.

How to read the equation: "Required return = risk-free rate + (the stock's beta × the market risk premium)." A risk-free stock (β = 0) needs only the risk-free rate. A market-like stock (β = 1) needs the full market return. Riskier-than-market stocks (β > 1) need more.

The equation defines a straight line called the Security Market Line (SML) in (β, required return) space. Slope = market risk premium. Intercept = KRF.

7b · CAPM example 1 · Find the required return

Given: KRF = 8%, KM = 12%, β = 2.

K_i = 8% + 2 × (12% − 8%) = 8% + 2 × 4% = 8% + 8% = 16%

Investors demand 16% on this high-beta stock. (β = 2 means it moves twice as much as the market.)

7c · CAPM example 2 · Find the beta

Given: Expected return = 12%, KRF = 4%, KM = 10%. Find β.

12% = 4% + β × (10% − 4%) 12% − 4% = β × 6% 8% = β × 6% β = 8% / 6% = 1.33

The stock's beta is 1.33. Solve the CAPM by isolating whichever variable is unknown — same equation, different rearrangement.

Cov(A, M)

Cov(B, M)

Course convention: use the magnitude of covariance when computing β here. Stock B's β = 5.45 — extremely sensitive to the market (much more volatile than market itself).

9 · Alpha (α) — accept or reject decision

CAPM tells us the required return for a given beta. But the stock's expected return (computed from its own probability distribution in §2) might be higher or lower than that required level. The difference is called alpha.

$$ \alpha_i \;=\; E[R_i] \;-\; K_i $$

Decision rule:

  • α > 0 (positive) → expected return exceeds required return → stock is underpriced / a bargain → ACCEPT (buy).
  • α < 0 (negative) → expected return falls short of required return → stock is overpriced → REJECT (don't buy / sell).
  • α = 0 → fairly priced → indifferent.

9a · Required returns (using KRF = 8%, KM = 12%)

Stock A's required return
K_A = 8% + 1.364 × (12% − 8%) = 8% + 1.364 × 4% = 8% + 5.456% = 13.456% ≈ 13.46%
Stock B's required return
K_B = 8% + 5.45 × (12% − 8%) = 8% + 5.45 × 4% = 8% + 21.8% = 29.8%

9b · Alpha calculations

αA
α_A = E[R_A] − K_A = 12.5% − 13.46% = −0.96% α_A < 0 → REJECT Stock A.
αB
α_B = E[R_B] − K_B = 20% − 29.8% = −9.8% α_B < 0 → REJECT Stock B.

Both stocks have negative alpha here. Stock A's expected return (12.5%) doesn't compensate for its market risk (which demands 13.46%). Stock B is even worse — 20% expected vs. 29.8% required. Both are overpriced relative to risk; pass on both.

10 · Exam traps (pulled from past finals)

  1. "Random diversification reduces total risk." → FALSE. It reduces only unsystematic (company-specific) risk. Total risk = systematic + unsystematic, and systematic remains.
  2. "Adding stocks to a portfolio reduces the portfolio's company-specific risk." → TRUE. Other choices ("reduces beta," "increases expected return," "increases overall risk," "increases systematic risk") are wrong. (Q1 on past final.)
  3. "Risk that cannot be diversified away is ___." → Systematic risk (also called market risk, non-diversifiable risk). Other choices: nonsystematic / non-market / total — all wrong. (Q16.)
  4. "Market risk is best measured by ___." → Beta. Not alpha (a return measure), not standard deviation (total risk), not coefficient of variation. (Q14.)
  5. "Standard deviation measures ___." → Total risk of a security. Not just systematic, not just unsystematic, not the equity risk premium. (Q18.)
  6. "Expected value is ___." → Weighted average of all possible outcomes. Not the inverse of standard deviation, not a correlation, not a probability distribution. (Q29.)
  7. "Portfolio weights are found by ___." → Calculating the percentage each asset is to total portfolio value. Not by dividing standard deviation by expected value, etc. (Q31.)
  8. "Portfolio expected return can be higher/lower than the weighted average of individual expected returns." → FALSE. It is always exactly the weighted average. (Variance can differ — that's where diversification works — but the expected return identity is rigid.) (Q23.)
  9. Risk-vs-return interpretation traps: if Stock B has higher E[R] than A but also higher σ, you can't say B is better — it depends on risk tolerance and on correlation with other holdings.
  10. CAPM application: given β, KRF, KM, compute Ki directly. Or rearrange to find any unknown given the others. Memorize the SML equation and three rearrangements (solve for K, β, KRF, or KM).
  11. Alpha decision rule. α = E[R] − K. Positive → ACCEPT, negative → REJECT. Same logic as Ch 9 §8: if expected exceeds required, the stock is a bargain.
  12. Beta interpretations: β = 1 means market-like; β > 1 means more volatile than market; β < 1 means less volatile; β < 0 (rare) means moves opposite to market — a hedge.

12 · Quick-recall flashcards

Click each card to reveal the answer.

What is expected value (E[X])?
The probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes: E[X] = Σ pi xi. NOT a simple average; each outcome is multiplied by its probability before summing.
What does variance measure?
The probability-weighted average of the squared deviations from the mean: σ² = Σ pi(xi − E[X])². It quantifies how much outcomes spread around the mean. Squared units; usually converted to standard deviation for interpretation.
Why do we square the deviations in variance?
Two reasons: (1) without squaring, positive and negative deviations would cancel and the average would always be 0. (2) Squaring penalizes large deviations more than small ones — appropriate for capturing risk.
Standard deviation?
σ = √σ². Square root of variance. Same units as the original variable. Interpreted as "typical deviation from the mean." Most common measure of total risk.
Covariance — what does the sign tell you?
Cov > 0 → variables move together (when one is high, the other tends to be high). Cov < 0 → variables move oppositely. Cov = 0 → no linear relationship. Magnitude is hard to interpret on its own (depends on units).
Correlation coefficient — range and meaning?
ρ = Cov(X,Y) / (σ_X · σ_Y). Always in [−1, +1]. ρ = +1 perfect positive (lockstep, same direction). ρ = 0 no linear relationship. ρ = −1 perfect negative (lockstep, opposite directions).
Portfolio expected return formula?
E[Rp] = Σ wi E[Ri]. Always EXACTLY the weighted average; never higher or lower. (Diversification affects variance, not expected return.)
Portfolio variance formula (2-asset)?
σ²p = w²Aσ²A + w²Bσ²B + 2wAwB·Cov(A,B). The 2wAwB·Cov term is what enables diversification — when Cov is negative, this term reduces total portfolio variance.
Why does diversification work?
Different stocks have different sensitivities to firm-specific events. Holding many stocks means firm-specific bad news on one stock is offset by firm-specific good news on another, on average. Market-wide risk (recession, etc.) cannot be diversified — it affects all stocks at once.
Systematic vs unsystematic risk?
Systematic = market risk = non-diversifiable. Affects all stocks. Measured by β. Unsystematic = company-specific = diversifiable. Eliminated by holding many stocks.
What does beta measure?
A stock's sensitivity to market-wide moves. β = Cov(R, RM) / σ²M. β = 1 → moves with market. β > 1 → more volatile than market. β < 1 → less volatile. β < 0 → moves opposite to market.
CAPM / SML equation?
Ki = KRF + βi(KM − KRF). The required return equals the risk-free rate plus beta times the market risk premium. Defines the Security Market Line.
What is the market risk premium?
KM − KRF. The extra return demanded for holding the (risky) market portfolio instead of the risk-free asset. Reward for taking systematic risk.
Alpha (α) and the accept/reject rule?
α = E[R] − K. Positive α → expected return exceeds required → ACCEPT (stock is underpriced). Negative α → REJECT (overpriced). Zero → fairly priced.
If a stock has β = 0, what does CAPM say its required return is?
K = KRF + 0 × premium = KRF. Same as the risk-free rate. (Makes sense — no systematic risk means no risk premium.)
What does it mean if E[Rp] equals the weighted average exactly but σp is much lower than the weighted average σ?
Diversification has reduced risk without reducing return. The negative-correlation example (75% A + 25% B → σp = 1.28%) illustrates this perfectly.
